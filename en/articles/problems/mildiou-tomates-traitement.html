Tomato Blight: How to Identify, Prevent and Treat Naturally

By iGardening Team Published March 12, 2026 20 min read

Tomato blight (Phytophthora infestans) is the most feared disease of vegetable gardeners worldwide. This devastating fungal-like organism can destroy an entire tomato crop in just a few days if conditions are right and no preventive measures are taken. The same pathogen that caused the Great Irish Famine of the 1840s continues to devastate home gardens and commercial crops today. But understanding how blight works, recognizing the first symptoms, and applying the right preventive and curative measures can save your harvest. This complete guide covers everything you need to know to identify, prevent, and treat tomato blight naturally.

Understanding Tomato Blight

What is blight?

Phytophthora infestans is not technically a true fungus but an oomycete -- a water mold that behaves like a fungus. It produces microscopic spores that are spread by wind and rain splash, traveling miles from infected plants. When spores land on wet tomato foliage, they germinate and penetrate the leaf tissue within hours, beginning the infection cycle.

Conditions that favor blight

Blight thrives in cool, wet weather. The ideal conditions for infection are: temperatures between 50 and 77 °F (10 to 25 °C), high humidity (above 75%), prolonged leaf wetness (more than 10 hours), and poor air circulation. This is why blight is most common during rainy periods in late summer and early autumn. Hot, dry weather stops blight dead in its tracks.

Identifying Blight Symptoms

On leaves

The first signs are dark, water-soaked patches on the leaves, usually starting at the tips and edges. These patches quickly expand and turn brown-black, often with a pale green or yellowish border. In humid conditions, a white fuzzy mold appears on the underside of the patches -- these are the sporulating structures producing millions of new spores. Affected leaves eventually blacken, shrivel, and die.

On stems

Dark brown to black lesions appear on stems, often at leaf junctions. Severely infected stems become brittle and may snap. If the main stem is girdled by a lesion, everything above it wilts and dies.

On fruits

Infected tomatoes develop firm, dark brown, irregular patches, often starting at the top near the stem attachment. The flesh beneath the patches turns brown and grainy. Green, unripe tomatoes are most susceptible. Already-red, ripe tomatoes are more resistant but can still be infected. Affected fruits quickly become inedible and rot completely.

Prevention: Your Best Defense

Preventing blight is infinitely easier than curing it. Here are the most effective preventive measures, listed in order of importance.

Choose resistant varieties

While no tomato variety is completely immune to blight, some show significantly better resistance. Look for varieties labeled as "blight-resistant" or "late blight tolerant." Notable resistant varieties include 'Ferline', 'Fantasio F1', 'Legend', 'Defiant', 'Mountain Magic', and 'Iron Lady'. Cherry tomatoes are generally more resistant than large-fruited varieties. Growing resistant varieties does not eliminate the need for other preventive measures but gives you a significant head start.

Ensure good air circulation

Space tomato plants at least 24 to 32 inches (60 to 80 cm) apart. Stake or cage them to keep foliage off the ground. Remove lower leaves up to the first fruit truss to improve airflow at the base. Prune suckers and thin excessive foliage. Good air circulation helps leaves dry quickly after rain or morning dew, reducing the window for spore germination.

Water correctly

Water at the base, never on the foliage. Drip irrigation or a soaker hose is ideal. Water in the morning so any accidentally splashed foliage dries quickly. Avoid overhead sprinklers completely for tomatoes.

Mulch generously

Apply a thick layer of mulch (4 to 6 inches / 10 to 15 cm) around the base of plants. Mulch prevents soil-borne spores from splashing up onto lower leaves during rain -- one of the primary infection routes. Straw, wood chips, or newspaper all work well.

Grow under cover

The single most effective prevention method: grow tomatoes under a rain shelter, open-sided greenhouse, or plastic tunnel. Keeping the foliage dry eliminates the main condition blight needs to infect. Many serious tomato growers in blight-prone regions consider a simple rain shelter an essential investment.

The Smith Period Warning

A "Smith Period" is a weather pattern of at least 48 consecutive hours where the temperature does not drop below 50 °F (10 °C) and humidity stays above 90% for at least 11 hours on each of those days. When a Smith Period is forecast, blight risk is very high. This is the time to apply preventive treatments immediately. Many gardening websites and apps now provide blight risk alerts based on local weather data.

Natural Treatments

Bordeaux mixture (copper sulfate + lime)

The classic preventive treatment, used since the 1880s. Bordeaux mixture creates a protective copper film on the foliage that prevents spore germination. Apply before infection occurs, ideally every 10 to 14 days from mid-June onwards, and after every heavy rain. It is a preventive, not a cure -- once blight has infected the tissue, copper cannot eliminate it. Follow dosage instructions carefully: excess copper is toxic to soil organisms and accumulates in the soil over time. Approved for organic gardening.

Baking soda spray

A gentler alternative to copper. Mix 1 tablespoon (5 g) of baking soda and 1 tablespoon of liquid soap (as a sticking agent) per quart (1 liter) of water. Spray on foliage every 7 to 10 days. The alkaline environment created on the leaf surface inhibits spore germination. Less effective than copper but harmless to the soil and completely non-toxic.

Horsetail decoction

Horsetail (Equisetum arvense) is rich in silica, which strengthens plant cell walls and makes them more resistant to fungal penetration. Boil 3.5 oz (100 g) of dried horsetail in 1 quart (1 liter) of water for 30 minutes. Dilute 1:5 and spray on foliage every 10 to 14 days as a preventive. Can be combined with baking soda for enhanced effect.

Milk spray

Dilute whole or skim milk 1:9 with water and spray on foliage weekly. The lactoferrin in milk has antifungal properties. Studies have shown milk spray to be surprisingly effective against various fungal diseases. Use fresh milk, not UHT.

Garlic infusion

Crush 3 to 4 cloves of garlic, steep in 1 quart (1 liter) of boiling water for 24 hours, strain, and add a drop of liquid soap. Spray on foliage. Garlic contains allicin, a compound with natural fungicidal and bactericidal properties.

What to Do When Blight Strikes

Act immediately

At the first sign of blight, remove all affected leaves and stems immediately. Cut well below the visible infection -- spores have often spread further than the visible damage. Place infected material directly in a sealed garbage bag -- never compost it, as spores survive composting. Wash your hands and tools with alcohol before touching healthy plants.

Harvest green tomatoes

If blight is spreading fast and you cannot control it, harvest all remaining tomatoes, including green ones. Uninfected green tomatoes will ripen indoors on a sunny windowsill or in a paper bag with a banana (which emits ethylene gas that promotes ripening). Sort through them daily and discard any showing symptoms.

End-of-season cleanup

After the season, remove and destroy (burn or trash) all tomato plant debris. Do not compost it. Blight spores can overwinter in plant debris and in the soil. Rotate your tomato growing location each year -- do not plant tomatoes (or potatoes, peppers, or eggplant, which are also susceptible) in the same spot for at least 3 years.

Monthly Prevention Calendar

March - April

Choose blight-resistant varieties when buying seeds or plants. Prepare your rain shelter or greenhouse if possible. Clean and disinfect stakes, cages, and tools from last season.

May - June

Plant out after last frost with proper spacing. Stake immediately. Apply first mulch layer. Begin preventive sprays (Bordeaux mixture or baking soda) from mid-June in blight-prone regions. Remove lower leaves as plants grow.

July - August

Peak blight risk period. Spray preventively every 10 to 14 days, and after every rainfall. Monitor plants daily for first symptoms. Maintain good airflow by pruning suckers and excess foliage. Water only at the base. Keep mulch topped up.

September - October

As temperatures drop and humidity rises, blight risk increases again. Continue preventive sprays. Harvest promptly as fruits ripen. At the first serious frost warning, harvest all remaining fruits including green ones. Clean up all plant debris thoroughly.

"The gardener who prevents blight is not the one who sprays the most, but the one who gives each plant the space to breathe, the shelter to stay dry, and the soil to grow strong."

Tomato blight is a formidable adversary, but it is far from unbeatable. With the right combination of resistant varieties, good cultural practices (spacing, airflow, ground-level watering, mulching), preventive natural treatments, and vigilant monitoring, you can enjoy a bountiful tomato harvest even in blight-prone regions. The key is consistent prevention rather than reactive treatment -- by the time blight is visible, it has already taken hold. Think of blight management as a season-long strategy rather than a single action, and your tomatoes will reward you with delicious, healthy fruit from summer through autumn.
