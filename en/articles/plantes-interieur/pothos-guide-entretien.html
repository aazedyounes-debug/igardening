Pothos (Epipremnum): The Unkillable Houseplant - Complete Guide

By iGardening Team Published March 12, 2026 20 min read

If there is one plant that deserves the title of "unkillable houseplant," it is the Pothos (Epipremnum aureum). This extraordinary tropical vine from the forests of Southeast Asia has won the hearts of millions of plant lovers worldwide with its cascading foliage, its ability to thrive in almost any condition, and its remarkable ease of propagation in water. Whether you are a complete beginner who has never kept a plant alive or a seasoned collector with dozens of rare specimens, the Pothos has something to offer you. This complete guide covers everything from choosing the perfect variety to advanced care techniques, water propagation, support training, and troubleshooting common issues.

The Pothos belongs to the Araceae family and has had a confusing taxonomic history -- you may see it labeled as Epipremnum aureum, Scindapsus aureus, or even Philodendron in some older references. In the wild, it is a vigorous climbing epiphyte that can reach 60 feet (20 meters) or more, with leaves growing to 3 feet (1 meter) long. Indoors, it remains much more modest but can easily produce trailing vines 6 to 10 feet (2 to 3 meters) long, making it perfect for high shelves, hanging baskets, or training up a moss pole.

The Most Popular Pothos Varieties

Golden Pothos

The original and most widely available variety. Its heart-shaped leaves are bright green splashed with irregular golden-yellow variegation. Incredibly hardy and forgiving, it tolerates low light, irregular watering, and neglect like no other. This is the perfect starter plant for anyone new to indoor gardening.

Marble Queen Pothos

A stunning variety with heavily marbled white and green foliage. Some leaves are almost entirely white, others mostly green, creating a striking, high-contrast display. Marble Queen grows more slowly than Golden Pothos due to its reduced chlorophyll and needs brighter light to maintain its pronounced variegation.

Neon Pothos

True to its name, this variety features solid chartreuse to neon yellow-green leaves that practically glow. It has no variegation, making it ideal for brightening dark corners -- though it will develop its most vivid color in bright indirect light. A refreshingly different look that pairs beautifully with darker-leaved plants.

N'Joy Pothos

A compact, slower-growing variety with smaller leaves sporting clean patches of white and green, quite distinct from the marbled pattern of Marble Queen. The variegation is more organized and graphic. N'Joy is perfect for small spaces and looks stunning in a hanging planter.

Cebu Blue Pothos

Perhaps the most unique-looking Pothos, with elongated, arrow-shaped leaves in a striking blue-green to silvery-green color. Unlike other Pothos, Cebu Blue has no variegation but its metallic sheen is utterly captivating. When given a support to climb, mature leaves develop fenestrations (natural holes) similar to a Monstera.

Pothos vs. Philodendron: How to Tell Them Apart

Pothos and heartleaf Philodendron (Philodendron hederaceum) are often confused. The easiest way to tell them apart: Pothos leaves are thicker and waxier with a slightly textured surface, while Philodendron leaves are thinner, smoother, and more heart-shaped. Pothos also has a slightly raised ridge along the midrib on the top of the leaf. New Pothos leaves unfurl from the previous leaf's base, while Philodendron new leaves emerge from a papery cataphyll sheath.

Light and Placement

Ideal light conditions

Pothos is famously tolerant of low light -- it can survive in offices with only fluorescent lighting and windowless bathrooms (with occasional trips to a brighter spot). However, "survive" is not the same as "thrive." For the best growth and most vivid variegation, provide bright indirect light. An east or north-facing window is ideal. Avoid direct afternoon sun, which can burn the leaves. Variegated varieties (Marble Queen, N'Joy) need more light than solid-colored ones (Golden, Neon) to maintain their patterns.

Can Pothos grow in water permanently?

Yes! Pothos is one of the few plants that can live indefinitely in water alone. Simply place cuttings in a glass jar or vase filled with water, keeping nodes submerged and leaves above the waterline. Change the water every 1 to 2 weeks to prevent stagnation and algae. Add a drop of liquid fertilizer monthly. Water-grown Pothos develop specialized water roots and will grow more slowly than soil-grown plants, but they make stunning, low-maintenance displays.

Watering and Humidity

Watering guidelines

Let the top inch (2 to 3 cm) of soil dry out between waterings. Pothos communicates clearly when it is thirsty: the leaves become slightly limp and droopy. Water thoroughly and they perk back up within hours. This drooping trick makes Pothos incredibly forgiving -- even if you forget to water for a while, it will recover beautifully. Overwatering (keeping soil constantly wet) is far more dangerous than underwatering, leading to root rot. In spring/summer, expect to water every 7 to 10 days; in winter, every 2 to 3 weeks.

Humidity

Pothos tolerates normal household humidity but grows lusher in higher humidity (50-70%). Brown leaf tips are often a sign of low humidity. Misting, pebble trays, and grouping plants together all help. Bathrooms and kitchens, with their naturally higher humidity, are excellent locations.

Soil, Fertilizing, and Repotting

Ideal soil mix

A standard indoor plant potting mix works well. For even better drainage, mix 70% potting soil with 30% perlite. Pothos are not fussy about soil as long as it drains well and does not stay waterlogged.

Fertilizing

Feed monthly during the growing season (spring through early autumn) with a balanced liquid fertilizer diluted to half strength. No fertilizer is needed in winter. Overfertilizing causes salt buildup that can burn roots and brown leaf edges. If in doubt, fertilize less rather than more.

Repotting

Repot every 1 to 2 years, or when roots fill the pot. Choose a pot 1 to 2 inches (2 to 5 cm) larger. Spring is the best time. Pothos actually grow well when slightly rootbound, so there is no rush to repot. Ensure the new pot has drainage holes.

Propagation: The Easiest Plant to Multiply

Pothos propagation is incredibly simple and is a wonderful way to create new plants, fill out a sparse pot, or share with friends.

Water propagation (the most popular method)

Cut a stem section 4 to 6 inches (10 to 15 cm) long, ensuring each cutting has at least one node (the small brown bump where roots emerge) and one or two leaves. Remove any leaves that would be submerged. Place in a glass of clean water with the nodes below the waterline. Place in bright indirect light. Change the water every 5 to 7 days. Roots will appear within 1 to 3 weeks. Once roots are 2 to 3 inches (5 to 8 cm) long, transplant into soil or leave in water permanently.

Soil propagation

Take the same type of cutting and plant directly into moist potting mix, burying the node(s). Keep the soil consistently moist (not wet) until new growth appears, indicating successful rooting. A clear plastic bag over the pot helps maintain humidity during rooting.

Training and Display Ideas

Hanging baskets and trailing

The most classic way to display Pothos. The vines cascade beautifully from hanging planters, high shelves, or the top of bookcases. For the fullest effect, start with several cuttings in the same pot and pinch growing tips regularly to encourage branching.

Climbing on a moss pole

In the wild, Pothos is a climber. When given a vertical support like a moss pole, coir pole, or trellis, Pothos produces increasingly larger leaves -- a dramatic transformation from the small trailing leaves. Secure the vines to the support with soft plant ties or pins. Keep the moss pole moist by misting to encourage aerial root attachment.

Living wall or curtain

Train long vines along hooks or command strips attached to the wall to create a living green curtain. This works beautifully along a window frame, across a bookshelf, or around a doorway.

Common Problems and Solutions

Yellow leaves

The most common cause is overwatering. Check the soil -- if it is soggy, let it dry out thoroughly. Other causes include too much direct sun, cold drafts, or natural aging of older leaves (which is normal).

Brown tips

Usually caused by low humidity, inconsistent watering, or salt buildup from fertilizer or hard water. Trim the brown tips with clean scissors, increase humidity, and flush the soil with water to remove salt deposits.

Loss of variegation

If variegated leaves are reverting to solid green, the plant needs more light. Move it closer to a bright window. You can also prune away reverted (all-green) stems to encourage new variegated growth.

Leggy growth with sparse leaves

This is a sign of insufficient light. Move to a brighter location and prune back the leggy vines to a few inches from the soil. The plant will produce new, bushier growth from the cut points.

Pests

Pothos is relatively pest-resistant but can occasionally attract mealybugs and spider mites. Wipe leaves regularly with a damp cloth. Treat infestations with insecticidal soap or neem oil.

"The Pothos is the proof that the best things in life are simple. Give it a jar of water and a spot near a window, and it will grow into a cascade of living green that brightens any space and lifts any mood."

The Pothos is much more than just a "beginner's plant" -- it is a genuinely beautiful, endlessly versatile, and remarkably resilient green companion that deserves a place in every home. From the stunning Marble Queen to the electric Neon, from a simple jar of water on the kitchen counter to a dramatic moss pole specimen, the possibilities are endless. Its forgiving nature means you can experiment, learn, and grow your confidence as a plant parent without fear of fatal mistakes. If the Pothos is your first plant, it certainly will not be your last -- welcome to the wonderful world of indoor gardening.
